Core Concepts

Trading is decision-making under uncertainty

Your goal is not to “be right.” Your goal is to repeatedly make decisions where the expected outcome is positive after costs, and where the losing path is survivable.

Analysis method: positioning + price action

A practical synthesis: positioning/volatility structure helps define the probability distribution and key zones, while realized price action decides timing and whether the market is accepting or rejecting those zones.

Technical analysis vs. positioning analysis (a false debate)

The corpus treats this debate as unnecessary: technical analysis guesses liquidity zones and probabilities from historical data, while positioning analysis increases confidence by explaining the meaning behind levels and the current market environment. Micro-cycles are driven by the volatility market; realized patterns still dictate timing.

Position sizing is the real strategy

Two traders can take the same entry and have opposite outcomes depending on sizing and exits. The Kelly criterion is a way to connect edge and payout to an “optimal” growth fraction. Most humans should use a fraction of Kelly to reduce drawdown risk.

Expected value beats “being right”

One explicit warning in the corpus: most traders fail not because their directional accuracy is low, but because they lack a consistent strategy and discipline, and they size poorly. A good decision can lose; a bad decision can win. The goal is positive expected value after costs.

Fair bet (why “neutral” doesn’t mean “safe”)

The “fair bet” idea is introduced as: when expected value over many bets is exactly zero, neither side has an edge. In trading, you still pay costs and you still face variance. So “fair” is not a comfort blanket; it’s the baseline you must beat.

Furus vs. analysts (how people get trapped)

A practical teaching in the corpus: novices prefer gurus because they give simple, actionable statements. Analysts often explain dynamics without a single “do this now” call. The trap is outsourcing thinking (copy-trading) instead of owning a process and validating claims.

How To Apply

A risk-first template for any trade

  1. Hypothesis: What must be true for this trade to work?
  2. Invalidation: Where is it clearly wrong?
  3. Risk: How much do you lose if invalidated?
  4. Reward: What is a realistic target (not the dream target)?
  5. Size: Choose size so loss is acceptable and repeatable.

Two “anti-ruin” rules

  • Never add risk to repair ego. If you want to “win it back,” your size is already too large.
  • Stop trading when the process breaks. A losing streak is information; enforce a stop rule.

Why “working people” need a different approach

If you have limited screen time, you can’t fight professionals on speed. You win by selecting fewer, higher-quality decisions, sizing safely, and avoiding emotional overtrading.

Common Mistakes

  • Changing your rules after a loss (reinforcing bad behavior).
  • Overtrading to “win it back.”
  • Confusing a good outcome with a good decision.
  • Taking small wins and large losses (negative expectancy disguised as activity).
  • Optimizing for accuracy instead of expectancy (risk/reward + base rates).
  • Copy-trading “furus” without understanding the model, costs, and risk constraints.

Self-Check

  1. Do you know your maximum loss per trade (in dollars) before you enter?
  2. Can you describe Kelly sizing in plain language?
  3. Can you explain the difference between technical analysis and positioning analysis?
  4. What is your rule for stopping after a losing streak?
  5. Can you explain what a “fair bet” is, and why costs make it negative for you?
